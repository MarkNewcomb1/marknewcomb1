Why aren't these mp3s? What's a flac file?


Mp3 is a lossy format that frankly should've died in the early 2000s. "Lossy" compression means you reduce the sound quality when you compress an audio file; not something I wish to do after recording the source files at a high resolution with good quality microphones and professional, expensive mic preamps. Some people claim that at a high enough bitrate you "can't tell the difference," but that's all subjective to the listener, the source file, the quality of the equipment they're listening on, etc. I'd rather remove an element of that altogether by just using a different format. Objectively, you ARE losing data with the mp3 format. I remember when people were claiming 128kbps mp3s were "CD quality". (In case you're wondering, to actually get to CD quality you'd have to rip an mp3 to 1,411kbps, but by then you're not compressing anything.)

FLAC is a lossless compression format, which means you still get all of the audio resolution (and therefore sound quality) of the original file that came out of the mastering studio, while reducing the file size from those original, huge uncompressed wav files. Are the flacs bigger than mp3s? Yes, but it's not 1998 anymore; people have largely moved on from 56K modems and 8GB hard drives on their computers.

It's time for mp3 to go. It was a fine solution for downloading 'N Sync from Napster back in the day, but now audio compression technology, internet speed, and hard drive storage have moved on.
